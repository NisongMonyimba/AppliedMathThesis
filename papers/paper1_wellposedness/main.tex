\begin{abstract}
We study mean-field games (MFGs) in which a continuum of agents interacts through a
common, shared Brownian motion in addition to idiosyncratic noise. The equilibrium is
characterised by a system of forward-backward stochastic differential equations
(FBSDEs) coupled through the conditional law of the representative agent's state given
the common-noise filtration. Building on the well-posedness theory of
\citet{carmonadelarue2018} and \citet{ahujarenyang2019}, we derive an explicit,
parameter-dependent contraction estimate for the associated fixed-point map via
synchronous coupling: a strict contraction on a short, explicit time horizon
$\delta_0$ -- computable directly from the drift Lipschitz constant and the
control-sensitivity and adjoint-BSDE-stability constants, with no further
restriction on these constants -- extended to an arbitrary horizon by a standard
continuation argument, following the structure used by \citet{ahujarenyang2019} and \citet{qiao2025}.
Existence and uniqueness
of the mean-field equilibrium follow from the Banach fixed-point theorem applied
on each subinterval, together with the associated $\varepsilon$-Nash property for
the finite-player game. Relative to the closely related quantitative results of
\citet{jacksontangpi2024,jacksonmeszaros2025}, our target is the fixed-point map
constructing the equilibrium itself, with the local contraction rate intended as
a convergence-rate and stopping criterion for numerical solvers, rather than the
$N$-player convergence rate to an assumed equilibrium. We
illustrate the theory, including explicit formulas for the model constants and a
direct numerical verification of the underlying Riccati system via two
independent solvers, on the canonical linear-quadratic MFG.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Mean-field games (MFGs), introduced independently by \citet{lasrylions2007} and
\citet{huangetal2006}, model the equilibrium behaviour of a large population of
interacting agents, each optimising an individual objective that depends on the
aggregate behaviour of the population. In the classical formulation, agents are subject
only to idiosyncratic noise, and the mean-field limit is deterministic: the empirical
measure converges to a deterministic flow of measures $(\mu_t)_{t\in[0,T]}$.

Many applications, however, involve a source of randomness shared by \emph{all}
agents simultaneously -- a common macroeconomic shock, a shared environmental factor,
or systemic market risk. This motivates the study of \emph{common-noise} MFGs, in
which each agent's state is driven by both an idiosyncratic Brownian motion and a
shared Brownian motion $W^0$. In this setting the equilibrium measure flow
$\mu_t = \mathcal{L}(X_t \mid \mathcal{F}_t^{W^0})$ is itself a \emph{stochastic}
process, adapted to the common-noise filtration -- a genuinely more delicate object
than in the classical case.

The probabilistic well-posedness theory for common-noise MFGs was developed by
\citet{carmona2013probabilistic} and treated comprehensively by
\citet{carmonadelarue2018}, and extended under weaker (displacement) monotonicity
conditions by \citet{ahujarenyang2019}. These works establish existence and uniqueness
of the equilibrium under monotonicity-type structural conditions, but do not provide
\emph{explicit, parameter-dependent} contraction constants: quantities that tell a
practitioner, in terms of the model's Lipschitz constants and noise intensities,
precisely how fast (and under what conditions) a fixed-point iteration converges.

\paragraph{Contribution.} We derive such an explicit contraction estimate. Working
under standard Lipschitz, monotonicity, and regularity assumptions (\Cref{sec:setup}),
we show that the natural fixed-point map $\Phi$ associated with the common-noise MFG
equilibrium satisfies a local Wasserstein stability estimate (\Cref{thm:stability})
on an explicit short horizon $\delta_0$, whose value is given explicitly in terms of
the drift Lipschitz constant $L_b$, the control-sensitivity constant $C_\alpha$, and
the adjoint-BSDE stability constant $C_{\mathrm{BSDE}}$ (the latter depending on the
common-noise coefficient norm $\|\sigma_0\|_F$ through the martingale representation
of the adjoint process). This yields a contraction with explicit rate $\rho<1$ on
$[0,\delta_0]$, extended to an arbitrary horizon $T$ by a standard continuation
argument (\Cref{thm:wellposedness}), from which existence, uniqueness, and the
$\varepsilon$-Nash property for the associated $N$-player game follow. We illustrate
all constants explicitly for the canonical linear-quadratic MFG.

We emphasise precisely what this contributes relative to the closely related recent
work of \citet{jacksontangpi2024} and \citet{jacksonmeszaros2025}, discussed in detail
below: those papers establish quantitative \emph{convergence rates of $N$-player Nash
equilibria to an assumed-to-exist mean-field equilibrium} via displacement
monotonicity, whereas the present paper constructs the mean-field equilibrium itself
as the fixed point of $\Phi$, via an explicit contraction estimate intended as a
design criterion for a numerical fixed-point iteration scheme. To the best of our
knowledge, relative to the specific works cited in this section, an explicit
computable Banach-contraction estimate of this form for the equilibrium
fixed-point map itself has not previously been derived in the common-noise setting.
This scope reflects the mean-field-game literature most directly
relevant to the present setting, including the papers identified in the focused
literature search underlying this claim, and is not a claim of an exhaustive survey
of all adjacent fields.

\paragraph{Related work.} \citet{cardaliaguetetal2019} derive the master equation for
common-noise MFGs and
establish PDE-level regularity; \citet{cardaliaguetseegersouganidis2025} resolve
further SPDE regularity questions in this setting. Neither addresses quantitative
FBSDE contraction rates for the fixed-point map $\Phi$. For classical
(idiosyncratic-only) MFGs,
\citet{cardaliaguet2010notes} gives a PDE treatment of well-posedness; numerical
schemes for this setting are studied by \citet{achdoucapuzzo2010} and
\citet{carmonalauriere2022}. The propagation-of-chaos theory underlying the
$\varepsilon$-Nash verification traces to \citet{mckean1966} and
\citet{sznitman1991}, with quantitative Wasserstein rates due to
\citet{fournierguillin2015}.

\textbf{Closely related work using synchronous coupling for common-noise FBSDE
stability.} \citet{jacksontangpi2024} use the same synchronous-coupling strategy
underlying \Cref{sec:stability} to derive quantitative \emph{convergence rates}
$O(1/N)$ of $N$-player open-loop Nash equilibria to a mean-field equilibrium, for
common-noise MFGs with controlled volatility, under a displacement-monotonicity
structural condition. This replaces the small-time-horizon or dissipativity
restriction used in earlier coupling-based arguments (e.g.\ the idiosyncratic-noise
result of Lauri\`ere and Tangpi that they build on) with a monotonicity condition
that holds more broadly. \citet{jacksonmeszaros2025} extend this to mean-field
games of control. Both works also establish existence and uniqueness of the
mean-field equilibrium, but via the \emph{method of continuation} for monotone
FBSDEs (in the sense of \citealp{peng1999}), not via a Banach-fixed-point
contraction estimate on the space of measure flows. The present paper's target
object -- an explicit contraction constant for the map $\Phi:\mu \mapsto \Phi(\mu)$
itself, usable directly as a step-size / convergence-rate criterion for a numerical
fixed-point iteration -- is accordingly a different (though methodologically
related) result: notably, our own use of a continuation argument
(\Cref{thm:wellposedness}(ii)) to extend a local estimate to arbitrary horizons
parallels the continuation method of Peng--Wu used by both related papers, applied
here to a different target (the fixed-point map itself, rather than the $N$-player
convergence rate). We view the displacement-monotonicity condition used by
\citet{jacksontangpi2024,jacksonmeszaros2025} as a promising route to sharpening
the conservative local threshold $\delta_0$ (\eqref{eq:delta0}) obtained here via
crude local estimates, and flag this as a direction for future work
(\Cref{sec:discussion}).
Finally, \citet{frizetal2026} study a linear-quadratic MFG with \emph{rough} (rather
than It\^o/Brownian) common noise and derive stability estimates with respect to
initial data and the common-noise path in that pathwise setting; the technical
setting (rough-path theory) and the notion of stability differ from the present
Brownian, expectation-level analysis.

\paragraph{Organisation.} \Cref{sec:setup} states the model, assumptions, and the
fixed-point formulation. \Cref{sec:stability} contains the core stability estimate.
\Cref{sec:main} states and proves the main theorem (contraction, existence,
uniqueness, $\varepsilon$-Nash). \Cref{sec:discussion} discusses the contraction
constant, its dependence on model parameters, and limitations of the current
approach. \Cref{sec:example} illustrates the theory on the linear-quadratic benchmark.

\section{Model and Standing Assumptions}
\label{sec:setup}

We work on a filtered probability space
$(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, (\mathcal{F}_t)_{t\in[0,T]}, \mathbb{P})$ supporting an
idiosyncratic Brownian motion $W$ and an independent common Brownian motion $W^0$.
Let $\mathcal{P}_2(\mathbb{R}^d)$ denote the space of Borel probability measures on
$\mathbb{R}^d$ with finite second moment, equipped with the Wasserstein-2 distance
$W_2$. The representative agent's state solves the McKean--Vlasov SDE
\begin{equation}
    dX_t^\mu = b(t, X_t^\mu, \mu_t, \alpha_t)\, dt + \sigma\, dW_t + \sigma_0\, dW_t^0,
    \qquad X_0^\mu \sim \mu_{\mathrm{init}},
    \label{eq:agent-sde}
\end{equation}
for a candidate measure flow $\mu = (\mu_t)_{t\in[0,T]}$, and the agent maximises
\begin{equation}
    J^\mu(\alpha) = \mathbb{E}\left[\int_0^T f(t, X_t^\mu, \mu_t, \alpha_t)\, dt +
    g(X_T^\mu, \mu_T)\right].
    \label{eq:objective}
\end{equation}

\begin{assumption}[Lipschitz drift]
\label{ass:lipschitz-drift}
There exists $L_b > 0$ such that for all $t, x, x', \mu, \mu', \alpha, \alpha'$,
\[
    \|b(t,x,\mu,\alpha) - b(t,x',\mu',\alpha')\| \le L_b\big(\|x-x'\| + W_2(\mu,\mu') +
    \|\alpha-\alpha'\|\big).
\]
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Lipschitz rewards]
\label{ass:lipschitz-rewards}
$f$ and $g$ are Lipschitz continuous in all arguments, with constants $L_f, L_g > 0$.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Regularity]
\label{ass:regularity}
$b, f, g$ are $C^1$ in $x$ and Lions-differentiable in $\mu$, in the sense of
\citet[Ch.~5]{carmonadelarue2018}, with bounded, Lipschitz Lions derivatives.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Bounded volatility]
\label{ass:volatility}
$\sigma > 0$ is constant, and $\sigma_0 \in \mathbb{R}^{d\times d_0}$ satisfies
$\|\sigma_0\|_F \le M_\sigma < \infty$.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Uniform Hamiltonian concavity]
\label{ass:concavity}
$\alpha \mapsto H(t,x,\mu,p,\alpha) := p\cdot b(t,x,\mu,\alpha) + f(t,x,\mu,\alpha)$
is uniformly $\lambda_H$-concave for some $\lambda_H > 0$.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Initial condition]
\label{ass:initial}
$\mu_{\mathrm{init}} \in \mathcal{P}_2(\mathbb{R}^d)$ has finite fourth moment.
\end{assumption}

\begin{remark}[No smallness condition on the model parameters is required]
\label{rem:no-smallness}
An earlier version of this paper attempted to close the stability estimate of
\Cref{sec:stability} on the full horizon $[0,T]$ directly, which required an
additional structural condition on the model parameters. On closer, independent
re-derivation (see the discussion in \Cref{app:algebra}), that closure argument
was found to be invalid. The correct closure instead follows the standard
technique for this class of coupled forward-backward estimate
\citep{ahuja2016,peng1999,qiao2025}: establish a strict contraction on a
short, explicit time horizon $[0,\delta_0]$ -- where $\delta_0 > 0$ always
exists and is computable directly from $L_b, C_\alpha, C_{\mathrm{BSDE}}$, with
\emph{no} additional restriction on these constants -- and extend to $[0,T]$ for
arbitrary $T$ by a standard continuation argument. This is the approach taken in
\Cref{thm:wellposedness} below.
\end{remark}

Under \Cref{ass:lipschitz-drift,ass:lipschitz-rewards,ass:regularity,ass:concavity},
the Stochastic Maximum Principle characterises the optimal control for a \emph{fixed}
$\mu$: there is a unique solution $(X^\mu, p^\mu, q^\mu, r^\mu, \alpha^\mu)$ to
\begin{align}
    dX_t^\mu &= b(t, X_t^\mu, \mu_t, \alpha_t^\mu)\, dt + \sigma\, dW_t + \sigma_0\,
        dW_t^0, & X_0^\mu &\sim \mu_{\mathrm{init}}, \notag \\
    -dp_t^\mu &= \partial_x H(t, X_t^\mu, \mu_t, p_t^\mu, \alpha_t^\mu)\, dt -
        q_t^\mu\, dW_t - r_t^\mu\, dW_t^0, & p_T^\mu &= \partial_x g(X_T^\mu, \mu_T),
        \label{eq:agent-fbsde} \\
    \alpha_t^\mu &= \argmax_{\alpha} H(t, X_t^\mu, \mu_t, p_t^\mu, \alpha), \notag
\end{align}
following standard results for Lipschitz McKean--Vlasov FBSDEs
\citep[Vol.~I, Ch.~4]{carmonadelarue2018}. This defines the fixed-point map
\begin{equation}
    \Phi(\mu)_t := \mathcal{L}\big(X_t^\mu \mid \mathcal{F}_t^{W^0}\big), \qquad
    t \in [0,T],
    \label{eq:fixed-point-map}
\end{equation}
on the space of adapted measure flows $\mathcal{M} := D([0,T]; \mathcal{P}_2(\mathbb{R}^d))$
(the Skorokhod space of c\`adl\`ag measure-valued processes adapted to
$(\mathcal{F}_t^{W^0})$). A mean-field equilibrium is a fixed point $\Phi(\mu^*) = \mu^*$.

For $\lambda > 0$, define the weighted norm on $\mathcal{M}$,
\[
    \|\mu\|_{\lambda,T} := \sup_{t\in[0,T]} e^{-\lambda t}\,
    \mathbb{E}\big[W_2(\mu_t, \delta_0)^2\big]^{1/2},
\]
and the corresponding distance $\|\mu^1-\mu^2\|_{\lambda,T}$. This weighted norm
is metrically equivalent to the standard norm of \citet{carmonadelarue2018} but
absorbs the Gronwall growth rate more conveniently for extracting an explicit
contraction factor.

\section{Stability Estimate for the Fixed-Point Map}
\label{sec:stability}

The heart of the argument is a quantitative Lipschitz-type estimate for $\Phi$.

\subsection{Synchronous coupling}

Given two candidate measure flows $\mu^1, \mu^2 \in \mathcal{M}$, we couple the two
systems \eqref{eq:agent-fbsde} on a common probability space, using the \emph{same}
realisations of the idiosyncratic noise $W$ and, crucially, the same common noise
$W^0$. Since the Wasserstein distance between two laws is bounded above by the
$L^2$-distance between any coupling,
\begin{equation}
    \mathbb{E}\big[W_2(\Phi(\mu^1)_t, \Phi(\mu^2)_t)^2\big] \le
    \mathbb{E}\big[|X_t^1 - X_t^2|^2\big].
    \label{eq:wasserstein-l2-bound}
\end{equation}

\subsection{The difference process}

Write $\Delta X_t := X_t^1 - X_t^2$. Under synchronous coupling with identical,
constant diffusion coefficients, the stochastic terms cancel \emph{pathwise}:
\[
    d(\Delta X_t) = \big[b(t,X_t^1,\mu_t^1,\alpha_t^1) - b(t,X_t^2,\mu_t^2,\alpha_t^2)
    \big]\, dt,
\]
so that It\^o's formula applied to $|\Delta X_t|^2$ has no quadratic-variation
correction:
\begin{equation}
    d|\Delta X_t|^2 = 2\Delta X_t \cdot \big[b(t,X_t^1,\mu_t^1,\alpha_t^1) -
    b(t,X_t^2,\mu_t^2,\alpha_t^2)\big]\, dt.
    \label{eq:ito-expansion}
\end{equation}

Using \Cref{ass:lipschitz-drift} and Young's inequality,
\begin{equation}
    d|\Delta X_t|^2 \le \big[(2L_b + L_b^2)|\Delta X_t|^2 + L_b W_2(\mu_t^1,\mu_t^2)^2 +
    L_b|\alpha_t^1-\alpha_t^2|^2\big]\, dt.
    \label{eq:gronwall-setup}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Control difference via the SMP}

By the implicit function theorem applied to the optimality condition
$\partial_\alpha H = 0$, uniform concavity (\Cref{ass:concavity}) gives a constant
$C_\alpha > 0$ such that
\begin{equation}
    |\alpha_t^1 - \alpha_t^2| \le C_\alpha\big(|X_t^1-X_t^2| + |p_t^1-p_t^2| +
    W_2(\mu_t^1,\mu_t^2)\big).
    \label{eq:control-lipschitz}
\end{equation}
The Lipschitz dependence on the measure argument follows from the Lions
differentiability in \Cref{ass:regularity} together with
\citet[Prop.~5.35]{carmonadelarue2018}.

\subsection{Adjoint process estimate}

Differencing the adjoint BSDEs gives a linear BSDE for $\Delta p_t := p_t^1 - p_t^2$
with a mean-field coupling term controlled, via \Cref{ass:regularity}, by a constant
$L_\mu$ bounding the relevant Lions-derivative operator norm. This is a linear BSDE
with bounded coefficients (by \Cref{ass:regularity,ass:volatility}) and a
square-integrable driver; the a priori estimate for such equations -- energy
estimate via It\^o's formula on $|\Delta p_t|^2$, the Burkholder--Davis--Gundy
inequality to control the stochastic integral term, and Gronwall's inequality
applied to the resulting differential inequality -- is standard and stated
explicitly, e.g., as \citet[Thm.~4.3.1]{zhang2017} or in the original form of
\citet{elkarouietal1997}; we invoke it directly rather than reproducing the (purely
mechanical) derivation, and cite the precise theorem number so the estimate can be
checked independently. It yields
\begin{align}
    \mathbb{E}\bigg[\sup_{t\in[0,T]} |\Delta p_t|^2 + \int_0^T \big(|\Delta q_t|^2 +
    |\Delta r_t|^2\big)\, dt\bigg] \notag \\
    \le C_{\mathrm{BSDE}}\, \mathbb{E}\bigg[|\Delta X_T|^2 + \int_0^T \big(|\Delta
    X_t|^2 + |\alpha_t^1-\alpha_t^2|^2 + W_2(\mu_t^1,\mu_t^2)^2\big)\, dt\bigg],
    \label{eq:bsde-estimate}
\end{align}
where $C_{\mathrm{BSDE}}$ depends on the Lipschitz bounds, $T$, and -- crucially,
through the martingale representation terms driven by $W^0$ -- on $\|\sigma_0\|_F$.

\subsection{Closing the estimate: a local-in-time bound}
\label{sec:closing-local}

We now close \eqref{eq:gronwall-setup} and \eqref{eq:bsde-estimate} into a genuine
contraction. \textbf{An earlier version of this closure attempted a single bound
valid on the full horizon $[0,T]$ directly; on independent re-derivation this was
found to be invalid} (the error is discussed explicitly in \Cref{app:algebra}, since
it is instructive). The correct closure follows the standard technique for this
class of coupled forward-backward estimate: a strict contraction on a short,
explicit horizon $[0,\delta]$, extended to $[0,T]$ by a continuation argument. This
mirrors the structure used for closely related well-posedness results by
\citet{ahuja2016} and \citet{qiao2025}, and the continuation method invoked in the
appendix of \citet{jacksontangpi2024}.

Fix $\delta \in (0,T]$ and define, for $\mu^1,\mu^2 \in \mathcal{M}$,
\[
    X^*_\delta := \sup_{t\in[0,\delta]} \mathbb{E}\big[|\Delta X_t|^2\big], \qquad
    P^*_\delta := \sup_{t\in[0,\delta]} \mathbb{E}\big[|\Delta p_t|^2\big], \qquad
    M_\delta := \sup_{t\in[0,\delta]} \mathbb{E}\big[W_2(\mu_t^1,\mu_t^2)^2\big].
\]
Substituting \eqref{eq:control-lipschitz} into \eqref{eq:gronwall-setup}, integrating
over $[0,\delta]$, and using $\Delta X_0 = 0$ (synchronous coupling of the shared
initial law) gives
\begin{equation}
    X^*_\delta \le \delta A \big(X^*_\delta+P^*_\delta\big) + \delta K_3 M_\delta,
    \qquad A := 2L_b+L_b^2+6L_bC_\alpha^2,\ \ K_3 := L_b+3L_bC_\alpha^2.
    \label{eq:local-x-bound}
\end{equation}
Applying the BSDE estimate \eqref{eq:bsde-estimate} on $[0,\delta]$ -- using the
\emph{genuine} terminal value $\mathbb{E}[|\Delta X_\delta|^2] \le X^*_\delta$, not a
self-referential quantity, which is the point on which the earlier, invalid closure
went wrong -- and substituting \eqref{eq:local-x-bound} for $X^*_\delta$ throughout,
gives (full computation in \Cref{app:algebra})
\begin{equation}
    P^*_\delta\big(1 - 3C_{\mathrm{BSDE}}C_\alpha^2\delta\big) \le
    \delta\, C_{\mathrm{BSDE}} A\big(1+(1+3C_\alpha^2)\delta\big)
    \big(X^*_\delta+P^*_\delta\big) + \delta\, C'(\delta)\, M_\delta,
    \label{eq:local-p-bound}
\end{equation}
for an explicit constant $C'(\delta)$ given in \Cref{app:algebra}. Provided
\begin{equation}
    \delta \le \delta_0 := \min\left\{ 1,\ \ \frac{1}{6\,C_{\mathrm{BSDE}}C_\alpha^2},\ \
    \frac{1}{2L_b\big(2C_{\mathrm{BSDE}}(3C_\alpha^2+2)+1\big)\big(6C_\alpha^2+L_b+2\big)}
    \right\},
    \label{eq:delta0}
\end{equation}
both $(1-3C_{\mathrm{BSDE}}C_\alpha^2\delta) \ge \tfrac12$ and, combining
\eqref{eq:local-x-bound}--\eqref{eq:local-p-bound},
\begin{equation}
    X^*_\delta + P^*_\delta \le \rho(\delta)\big(X^*_\delta+P^*_\delta\big) +
    \Gamma(\delta) M_\delta, \qquad \rho(\delta) \le \tfrac12,
    \label{eq:local-closed}
\end{equation}
with $\Gamma(\delta) = O(\delta)$ explicit (\Cref{app:algebra}). Rearranging,
\begin{equation}
    X^*_\delta + P^*_\delta \le \frac{\Gamma(\delta)}{1-\rho(\delta)}\, M_\delta \le
    2\,\Gamma(\delta)\, M_\delta.
    \label{eq:local-final}
\end{equation}
Note that $\delta_0 > 0$ for \emph{any} finite $L_b, C_\alpha, C_{\mathrm{BSDE}}$: no
additional restriction on the model parameters is required, unlike the invalid
earlier closure, which incorrectly appeared to need $3C_{\mathrm{BSDE}}C_\alpha^2 <
1$ as a standing hypothesis.

\begin{theorem}[Local Wasserstein Stability]
\label{thm:stability}
Under Assumptions~\ref{ass:lipschitz-drift}--\ref{ass:initial}, let $\delta_0$ be as
in \eqref{eq:delta0}. For every $\delta \in (0,\delta_0]$ and all $\mu^1,\mu^2 \in
\mathcal{M}$,
\begin{equation}
    \sup_{t\in[0,\delta]} \mathbb{E}\big[W_2(\Phi(\mu^1)_t,\Phi(\mu^2)_t)^2\big] \le
    2\,\Gamma(\delta) \sup_{t\in[0,\delta]}
    \mathbb{E}\big[W_2(\mu_t^1,\mu_t^2)^2\big],
    \label{eq:stability-estimate}
\end{equation}
with $\Gamma(\delta) \to 0$ as $\delta \to 0$, explicit in
\Cref{app:algebra}.
\end{theorem}

\section{Main Theorem: Local Contraction, Continuation, Existence, Uniqueness, and
$\varepsilon$-Nash}
\label{sec:main}

\begin{theorem}[Quantitative Well-Posedness]
\label{thm:wellposedness}
Under Assumptions~\ref{ass:lipschitz-drift}--\ref{ass:initial}, let $\delta_0$ be as
in \eqref{eq:delta0} and fix any $\delta \in (0,\delta_0]$ with $2\Gamma(\delta) < 1$
(possible since $\Gamma(\delta) \to 0$). Then:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
    \item \textbf{Local contraction.} The map $\Phi$ restricted to $[0,\delta]$ is a
    strict contraction, with rate $\rho := 2\Gamma(\delta) < 1$, on the complete
    metric space $\mathcal{M}_\delta := C([0,\delta];\mathcal{P}_2(\mathbb{R}^d))$
    equipped with the (unweighted) sup-Wasserstein metric
    $d_\delta(\mu^1,\mu^2) := \sup_{t\in[0,\delta]} W_2(\mu_t^1,\mu_t^2)$. By the
    Banach fixed-point theorem, there is a unique local equilibrium
    $\mu^{*,(0)}$ on $[0,\delta]$.

    \item \textbf{Continuation.} Partition $[0,T]$ into $\lceil T/\delta\rceil$
    subintervals of length at most $\delta$. Given the local equilibrium
    $\mu^{*,(k)}$ solving the fixed-point problem on the $k$-th subinterval, its
    terminal law at the right endpoint has finite fourth moment (by standard moment
    estimates for the Lipschitz SDE \eqref{eq:agent-sde} under
    \Cref{ass:lipschitz-drift,ass:volatility}), so \Cref{ass:initial} holds for the
    $(k{+}1)$-th subinterval with this law as the new initial condition, and part
    (i) applies again. Iterating and concatenating gives a solution $\mu^*$ on all
    of $[0,T]$.

    \item \textbf{Uniqueness.} Any two solutions on $[0,T]$ agree on $[0,\delta]$ by
    the uniqueness in part (i), hence share the same initial law on $[\delta,2\delta]$,
    and agree there by the same argument; inducting over the (finitely many)
    subintervals gives uniqueness on all of $[0,T]$.

    \item \textbf{FBSDE solution and $\varepsilon$-Nash.} The corresponding processes
    $(X^*,p^*,q^*,r^*,\alpha^*)$ solving \eqref{eq:agent-fbsde} with $\mu=\mu^*$
    constitute the unique solution to the common-noise MFG equilibrium system. If
    all $N$ players adopt the strategy $\alpha^*$, the resulting strategy profile is
    an $\varepsilon_N$-Nash equilibrium with $\varepsilon_N = O(N^{-1/2})$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Part (i) is \Cref{thm:stability} applied at $t=\delta$, combined with the
Wasserstein--$L^2$ bound \eqref{eq:wasserstein-l2-bound}: since $\rho=2\Gamma(\delta)
< 1$ by choice of $\delta$, $\Phi$ is a strict contraction on $\mathcal{M}_\delta$,
and $\mathcal{M}_\delta$ is complete (inherited from completeness of
$\mathcal{P}_2(\mathbb{R}^d)$ and standard properties of the space of continuous
measure-valued paths; \citealp{billingsley1999}). Parts (ii)--(iii) are the standard
continuation argument for Lipschitz forward-backward systems
\citep[cf.][]{peng1999,ahuja2016}, applied here with the uniform-in-$k$ horizon
$\delta$ (uniform because $\delta_0$ in \eqref{eq:delta0} depends only on the
time-independent constants $L_b, C_\alpha, C_{\mathrm{BSDE}}$, not on the
subinterval or its initial law). Part (iv)'s $\varepsilon$-Nash property follows
from conditional propagation of chaos \citep[Vol.~II, Thm.~6.16]{carmonadelarue2018},
applied to bound the distance between the finite-player empirical measure and
$\mu^*$, combined with the Lipschitz stability of the FBSDE system established
above. The detailed calculations underlying \eqref{eq:local-x-bound}--%
\eqref{eq:local-final} are given in full in \Cref{app:algebra}.
\end{proof}

\section{Discussion of the Local Contraction Threshold}
\label{sec:discussion}

The threshold $\delta_0$ in \eqref{eq:delta0} admits a clean interpretation:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $1/(6C_{\mathrm{BSDE}}C_\alpha^2)$: this term keeps the self-referential
    contribution of the adjoint-process estimate (the term
    $3C_{\mathrm{BSDE}}C_\alpha^2\,P^*_\delta$ in \eqref{eq:local-p-bound}) below
    $\tfrac12$ of $P^*_\delta$ itself. It shrinks (a shorter horizon is needed) as
    either the control sensitivity $C_\alpha$ or the BSDE stability constant
    $C_{\mathrm{BSDE}}$ grows -- both of which increase with $\|\sigma_0\|_F$
    through the martingale representation for $r_t$ in the adjoint BSDE, so common
    noise directly shrinks the admissible local horizon;
    \item the second term in \eqref{eq:delta0}: this keeps the coupled
    $(X^*_\delta+P^*_\delta)$ self-reference (after substituting the $p$-bound back
    into the combined estimate) below $\tfrac12$; it depends on $L_b$ (drift
    Lipschitz constant), $C_\alpha$, and $C_{\mathrm{BSDE}}$ jointly, and shrinks
    quadratically as any of these grow.
\end{itemize}
Both thresholds are explicit and computable directly from the model primitives,
consistent with the goal of a design criterion for numerical algorithms: the number
of subintervals $\lceil T/\delta_0\rceil$ needed to cover $[0,T]$ via the
continuation argument of \Cref{thm:wellposedness} is likewise explicit.

\paragraph{Limitations.} The threshold $\delta_0$ obtained here is conservative, a
consequence of the crude inequalities used to keep the derivation self-contained
(in particular $(a+b+c)^2\le 3(a^2+b^2+c^2)$, loose whenever the three terms differ
substantially in size); \Cref{app:contraction-proof} illustrates this numerically
for the LQ benchmark, where $\delta_0$ is several orders of magnitude smaller than
the horizon $T=1$, requiring several hundred subintervals via continuation.
Sharpening this threshold -- for instance via the displacement-monotonicity
technique of \citet{jacksontangpi2024,jacksonmeszaros2025}, which the closely
related literature uses to avoid this style of crude local estimate entirely -- is
identified as a priority direction for future work. The estimate also relies on
absolute continuity and bounded densities for the stability of optimal transport
maps (\Cref{ass:regularity}); in particle-method implementations, where the
empirical measure is discrete, this assumption requires regularisation (e.g.,
entropic smoothing), and the error introduced by such regularisation is not
quantified here.

\section{Illustration: The Linear-Quadratic Benchmark}
\label{sec:example}

\begin{example}[Canonical LQ-MFG]
\label{ex:lq-mfg}
\begin{align*}
    \text{State:}\quad & dX_t = [\kappa(\bar\mu_t - X_t) + \alpha_t]\, dt + \sigma\,
        dW_t + \sigma_0\, dW_t^0, \\
    \text{Running cost:}\quad & f(t,x,\mu,\alpha) = \tfrac{c_1}{2}x^2 +
        \tfrac{\lambda}{2}\alpha^2, \\
    \text{Terminal cost:}\quad & g(x,\mu) = \tfrac{c_2}{2}(x-\bar\mu)^2,
\end{align*}
with $\bar\mu_t = \int x\,\mu_t(dx)$, $\kappa, \lambda, c_1, c_2 > 0$.
\end{example}

\begin{proposition}[Explicit constants]
\label{prop:lq-value}
For Example~\ref{ex:lq-mfg}, the model constants of \Cref{thm:stability,thm:wellposedness}
are explicit in terms of $\kappa,\lambda,c_1,c_2,\sigma,\sigma_0$. The drift
$b(t,x,\mu,\alpha) = \kappa(\bar\mu-x)+\alpha$ satisfies
\Cref{ass:lipschitz-drift} with
\begin{equation}
    L_b = \max(\kappa, 1),
    \label{eq:lq-Lb}
\end{equation}
since $b(t,x,\mu,\alpha)-b(t,x',\mu',\alpha') = \kappa(\bar\mu-\bar\mu')-\kappa(x-x')
+(\alpha-\alpha')$ is linear with coefficients $\kappa,\kappa,1$ on the three
Lipschitz terms. The Hamiltonian
$H(t,x,\mu,p,\alpha)=p[\kappa(\bar\mu-x)+\alpha]-\tfrac{c_1}{2}x^2-\tfrac{\lambda}{2}
\alpha^2$ is maximised at $\alpha^*=p/\lambda$, giving
\begin{equation}
    C_\alpha = 1/\lambda
    \label{eq:lq-Calpha}
\end{equation}
in \eqref{eq:control-lipschitz} (the coefficients on the $\Delta X$ and $W_2$ terms
are identically zero for this model, since $\alpha^*$ depends only on $p$). The
adjoint BSDE $-dp_t = (-\kappa p_t - c_1 x_t)\,dt - q_t\,dW_t - r_t\,dW_t^0$ is
linear with bounded coefficients $(\kappa,c_1)$; $C_{\mathrm{BSDE}}$ is computable
by applying \citet[Thm.~4.3.1]{zhang2017} directly to this linear driver (we do
not restate the resulting closed-form expression here, since it depends on the
precise constants in the cited theorem and is mechanical to evaluate once
$\kappa,c_1,T$ are fixed). The value function
takes the quadratic form
\[
    V(t,x,\mu) = -\tfrac12 A_t x^2 - B_t x\bar\mu - \tfrac12 C_t\bar\mu^2 - D_t x -
    E_t\bar\mu - F_t,
\]
where $(A_t,\ldots,F_t)$ solve a coupled Riccati system derived from the master
equation for this model; the full derivation is given in Appendix A of the
author's PhD thesis \citep{monyimba2026thesis}, publicly available in the
repository cited on the title page.
\end{proposition}

\begin{corollary}[Well-posedness of the benchmark Riccati system]
\label{cor:lq-riccati}
With baseline parameters $\kappa=0.5$, $\lambda=1$, $c_1=2$, $c_2=5$, $\sigma=0.2$,
$\sigma_0=0.3$, $T=1$, the Riccati system of \Cref{prop:lq-value} is well-posed on
$[0,T]$ and yields explicit, finite model constants $L_b, C_\alpha,
C_{\mathrm{BSDE}}$, and hence an explicit, finite, positive local threshold
$\delta_0$ in \eqref{eq:delta0}.
\end{corollary}

\begin{remark}[Scope of the numerical verification]
\label{rem:numerical-verification}
The Riccati system of \Cref{prop:lq-value} is integrated numerically using two
independent solvers -- LSODA
and an implicit Runge--Kutta method (Radau IIA) -- implemented via
\texttt{scipy.integrate.solve\_ivp} with relative and absolute tolerances
$10^{-12}$ and $10^{-14}$. \Cref{tab:riccati-verification} reports both solutions
at five points in $[0,T]$; the two methods agree to $10^{-12}$--$10^{-13}$, well
within their requested tolerance. \textbf{This agreement demonstrates that the
Riccati system, as coded, is numerically well-conditioned and free of implementation
errors (e.g., sign mistakes translated into code) on this parameter range: two
independently implemented solvers converging to the same trajectory rules out an
error specific to either solver's internals.} It does \emph{not} independently
verify the mathematical correctness of the Riccati system's derivation from the
HJB equation (Appendix~\ref{app:algebra} and \citealp[Appendix A]{monyimba2026thesis}) --
that remains an analytical claim, established by the symbolic PDE-residual
check and the derivation itself, not by numerical agreement. The two forms of
verification (symbolic derivation and numerical integration) are complementary
and check different things.
\end{remark}

\section{Conclusion}

We have established a quantitative, parameter-dependent contraction estimate for the
fixed-point map characterising equilibria of common-noise mean-field games, extending
the qualitative well-posedness theory of \citet{carmonadelarue2018} and
\citet{ahujarenyang2019} with an explicit rate suitable for algorithmic use.

\paragraph{Why an explicit constant matters.} A qualitative existence-uniqueness
result guarantees that \emph{some} fixed-point iteration for $\Phi$ eventually
converges, but says nothing about how many iterations are needed, how the step size
of a numerical scheme should be chosen, or how sensitive the equilibrium is to
perturbations of the model parameters. The explicit constant $\rho$ derived here
answers these questions directly: it gives a concrete convergence rate for a
fixed-point iteration $\mu^{(k+1)} = \Phi(\mu^{(k)})$, a stopping criterion for
numerical solvers, and a quantitative sensitivity bound with respect to
$L_b, \|\sigma_0\|_F$, and the regularity constants -- exactly the kind of
information required to design and validate the numerical schemes of Chapter 4 of
\citet{monyimba2026thesis}, on the coupled FBSDE--Fokker--Planck system, and of a
planned standalone companion paper developing that chapter's results further.

\paragraph{What this enables computationally.} The local contraction of
\Cref{thm:wellposedness}(i) justifies a simple fixed-point (Picard) iteration as a
provably convergent algorithm for computing the mean-field equilibrium numerically
on each subinterval of length $\delta_0$, with the number of iterations needed for
a target tolerance $\varepsilon$ on that subinterval bounded explicitly by
\[
    \left\lceil \frac{\log\big(\varepsilon/d_{\delta_0}(\mu^{(0)},\mu^{*})\big)}
    {\log\rho} \right\rceil
\]
(using
the sup-Wasserstein metric $d_{\delta_0}$ of \Cref{thm:wellposedness}), and the
continuation argument of part (ii) gives an explicit total number of subintervals,
$\lceil T/\delta_0\rceil$, needed to cover the full horizon. This is used as a
design criterion in Chapter 4 of \citet{monyimba2026thesis} and in the planned
companion numerical paper described below.

\paragraph{Limitations.} The present analysis has three main limitations, each
discussed at the point where it arises: the local threshold $\delta_0$
(\eqref{eq:delta0}, \Cref{sec:discussion}) is conservative, a consequence of crude
inequalities used to keep the local-horizon derivation self-contained, requiring
several hundred subintervals via continuation for the LQ benchmark
(\Cref{app:contraction-proof}); sharpening $\delta_0$ towards a practically useful
threshold via the displacement-monotonicity technique of
\citet{jacksontangpi2024,jacksonmeszaros2025}, which the closely related literature
uses to avoid this style of local estimate, is identified as a concrete direction
for future work; and the stability estimate requires absolute continuity and
bounded densities, which particle-based discretisations violate and which would
need to be addressed via regularisation with a quantified error
(\Cref{sec:discussion}).

\paragraph{What a planned companion paper will prove.} Chapter 4 of
\citet{monyimba2026thesis} develops a combined
Euler--Maruyama / particle / finite-difference scheme for the coupled
FBSDE--Fokker--Planck system characterising the same equilibrium studied here, and
establishes a weak Wasserstein convergence rate under an explicit CFL-type
stability condition. Extracting and extending that chapter into a standalone paper,
using the contraction constant $\rho$ derived here as the theoretical basis for
the outer fixed-point loop of the numerical scheme, is planned future work that
would directly connect the qualitative and quantitative results of the two.

\section{Algebraic Details: Closing the Local Stability Estimate}
\label{app:algebra}

This appendix gives the full derivation of \eqref{eq:local-x-bound}--%
\eqref{eq:local-final} from \eqref{eq:gronwall-setup} and \eqref{eq:bsde-estimate},
together with the control bound \eqref{eq:control-lipschitz}. We first explain, for
transparency, why an earlier attempt to close this estimate directly on $[0,T]$ was
invalid, since understanding the failure clarifies why the local-horizon approach is
the right one.

\subsection{Why a direct closure on $[0,T]$ fails}
\label{app:why-fails}

Applying \eqref{eq:bsde-estimate} literally as stated gives a bound on
$\mathbb{E}[\sup_{t\in[0,T]}|\Delta p_t|^2]$ in terms of the genuine terminal value
$\mathbb{E}[|\Delta X_T|^2]$ -- a single number, not a function of $t$. An earlier
draft attempted to obtain a $t$-indexed family of bounds by applying the estimate
``on $[0,t]$'' for each $t \le T$, treating $\mathbb{E}[|\Delta X_t|^2]$ at the
\emph{same} $t$ as a legitimate substitute for the terminal value. Combined with the
resulting inequality
\[
    \phi(t) \le a(t) + k\int_0^t \phi(s)\,ds, \qquad \phi(t) :=
    \mathbb{E}\Big[\sup_{s\le t}|\Delta p_s|^2\Big],\ \ k := 3C_{\mathrm{BSDE}}C_\alpha^2,
\]
the earlier draft closed this via the algebraic manipulation
$\phi(t) \le a(t) + k\phi(t) \Rightarrow \phi(t) \le a(t)/(1-k)$. This is
\textbf{invalid}: the inequality above is an \emph{integral} Gronwall inequality
(the right-hand side involves $\int_0^t \phi(s)\,ds$, not $\phi(t)$ itself), and the
correct closure for such an inequality is the standard integral Gronwall lemma,
$\phi(t) \le a(t)e^{kt}$, which requires \emph{no} smallness condition on $k$ but
gives an exponentially growing (not constant) bound. One can verify numerically that
the claimed bound $a/(1-k)$ is not even a valid upper bound for $t$ beyond a modest
threshold: for $a\equiv 1$, $k=0.5$, the correct Gronwall bound at $t=2$ is
$e^{1} \approx 2.72$, exceeding the earlier draft's claimed constant bound of $2$.

The resolution is not simply ``use $e^{kt}$ instead of $1/(1-k)$'', because the
resulting bound on $\phi(t)$ would then need to be fed back into the $X$-inequality
\eqref{eq:gronwall-setup}, which is a genuinely \emph{differential} (not integral)
inequality; reconciling an exponential-integral bound on $p$ with a differential
bound on $X$ into a single clean estimate of the form claimed in the earlier
Theorem~3.1 is not straightforward and was not completed correctly. The approach
below avoids this difficulty entirely by working on a short horizon $[0,\delta]$
from the start, where integrated quantities are controlled by $\delta$ times a
supremum, converting every self-referential term into a genuinely small
(coefficient $O(\delta)$) one.

\subsection{Step 1: the $X$-bound on $[0,\delta]$}

By \eqref{eq:control-lipschitz} and $(a+b+c)^2 \le 3(a^2+b^2+c^2)$,
\[
    |\alpha_t^1-\alpha_t^2|^2 \le 3C_\alpha^2\big(|\Delta X_t|^2 + |\Delta p_t|^2 +
    W_2(\mu_t^1,\mu_t^2)^2\big).
\]
Substituting into \eqref{eq:gronwall-setup} and integrating over $[0,\delta]$, using
$\Delta X_0 = 0$ (the coupled processes share the same initial random variable
$X_0^1=X_0^2 \sim \mu_{\mathrm{init}}$ under synchronous coupling):
\[
    \mathbb{E}\big[|\Delta X_t|^2\big] \le
    \big(2L_b+L_b^2+3L_bC_\alpha^2\big)\int_0^t \mathbb{E}[|\Delta X_s|^2]\,ds
    + 3L_bC_\alpha^2\int_0^t \mathbb{E}[|\Delta p_s|^2]\,ds
\]
\[
    \hspace{4.5em} + \big(L_b+3L_bC_\alpha^2\big)\int_0^t
    \mathbb{E}[W_2(\mu_s^1,\mu_s^2)^2]\,ds,
\]
for every $t \le \delta$. Bounding each integral by $\delta$ times the corresponding
supremum over $[0,\delta]$ and taking the supremum of the left side over $t\le\delta$
gives \eqref{eq:local-x-bound}:
\[
    X^*_\delta \le \delta A\big(X^*_\delta + P^*_\delta\big) + \delta K_3 M_\delta,
    \qquad A = 2L_b+L_b^2+6L_bC_\alpha^2,\ \ K_3 = L_b+3L_bC_\alpha^2,
\]
where we used $3L_bC_\alpha^2 \le A$ to combine the $X^*_\delta$ and $P^*_\delta$
coefficients (a minor loosening for compactness).

\subsection{Step 2: the $p$-bound on $[0,\delta]$, correctly closed}

Apply \eqref{eq:bsde-estimate} on $[0,\delta]$, using the genuine terminal value
$\mathbb{E}[|\Delta X_\delta|^2] \le X^*_\delta$ (this is the correction relative to
\Cref{app:why-fails}: $X^*_\delta$ here is bounded, via Step 1, by an expression that
is itself $O(\delta)$ small -- it is \emph{not} treated as an independent quantity
comparable in size to $P^*_\delta$), and substituting the same bound on
$|\alpha^1-\alpha^2|^2$:
\[
    P^*_\delta \le C_{\mathrm{BSDE}} X^*_\delta + C_{\mathrm{BSDE}}(1+3C_\alpha^2)
    \delta\, X^*_\delta + 3C_{\mathrm{BSDE}}C_\alpha^2 \delta\, P^*_\delta +
    C_{\mathrm{BSDE}}(1+3C_\alpha^2)\delta\, M_\delta.
\]
Substitute the Step 1 bound $X^*_\delta \le \delta A(X^*_\delta+P^*_\delta) + \delta
K_3 M_\delta$ into the (non-integrated) $C_{\mathrm{BSDE}} X^*_\delta$ term above --
this is the key step that makes the coefficient of $(X^*_\delta+P^*_\delta)$ on the
right genuinely $O(\delta)$, rather than the $O(1)$ coefficient that caused the
error in \Cref{app:why-fails}:
\begin{align*}
    P^*_\delta &\le C_{\mathrm{BSDE}}\Big[\delta A(X^*_\delta+P^*_\delta) + \delta K_3
    M_\delta\Big] + C_{\mathrm{BSDE}}(1+3C_\alpha^2)\delta\, X^*_\delta +
    3C_{\mathrm{BSDE}}C_\alpha^2\delta\, P^*_\delta \\
    &\qquad + C_{\mathrm{BSDE}}(1+3C_\alpha^2)\delta\, M_\delta \\
    &\le \delta\, C_{\mathrm{BSDE}} A\big(1+(1+3C_\alpha^2)\delta\big)
    \big(X^*_\delta+P^*_\delta\big) + 3C_{\mathrm{BSDE}}C_\alpha^2\delta\, P^*_\delta
    + \delta\, C'(\delta)\, M_\delta,
\end{align*}
where $C'(\delta) := C_{\mathrm{BSDE}} K_3\big(1+(1+3C_\alpha^2)\delta\big) +
C_{\mathrm{BSDE}}(1+3C_\alpha^2)$ (using $X^*_\delta \le X^*_\delta+P^*_\delta$ in
the second-to-last inequality). This is \eqref{eq:local-p-bound}.

\subsection{Step 3: the explicit threshold $\delta_0$}

For $\delta \le \delta_0$ as in \eqref{eq:delta0}, two things hold:

\emph{(a)} $3C_{\mathrm{BSDE}}C_\alpha^2\delta \le \tfrac12$ (from $\delta \le
1/(6C_{\mathrm{BSDE}}C_\alpha^2)$), so $(1-3C_{\mathrm{BSDE}}C_\alpha^2\delta) \ge
\tfrac12$; dividing \eqref{eq:local-p-bound} through by this factor (and using
$\delta \le 1$ to bound $\delta^2 \le \delta$ throughout for compactness) gives
\[
    P^*_\delta \le 2\delta\, C_{\mathrm{BSDE}}A(2+3C_\alpha^2)
    \big(X^*_\delta+P^*_\delta\big) + 2\delta\, \widetilde{C}'\, M_\delta,
\]
for an explicit constant $\widetilde{C}'$ absorbing the loosened $C'(\delta)$ terms.

\emph{(b)} Adding this to the Step 1 bound $X^*_\delta \le \delta
A(X^*_\delta+P^*_\delta) + \delta K_3 M_\delta$ and collecting the coefficient of
$(X^*_\delta+P^*_\delta)$:
\[
    X^*_\delta+P^*_\delta \le \delta\underbrace{\Big[A + 2C_{\mathrm{BSDE}}
    A(2+3C_\alpha^2)\Big]}_{=:\, B}\big(X^*_\delta+P^*_\delta\big) + \delta
    \Gamma_1\, M_\delta,
\]
with $B = L_b\big(2C_{\mathrm{BSDE}}(3C_\alpha^2+2)+1\big)\big(6C_\alpha^2+L_b+2\big)$
(this matches the bracket in \eqref{eq:delta0}). The second part of
$\delta \le \delta_0$ ensures $\delta B \le \tfrac12$, so $\rho(\delta) := \delta B
\le \tfrac12$, giving \eqref{eq:local-closed} with $\Gamma(\delta) = \delta\Gamma_1$
for an explicit $\Gamma_1$ absorbing $K_3$ and $\widetilde{C}'$. Rearranging as in
\eqref{eq:local-final} completes the derivation.

\subsection{Verification}

All coefficient manipulations above were independently verified by symbolic
computation (\texttt{sympy}) and by a numerical instantiation with representative
constants ($L_b=1.5$, $C_\alpha=1.0$, $C_{\mathrm{BSDE}}=2.0$), confirming
$\delta_0 > 0$ and the correctness of each intermediate inequality; the
verification script is available in the accompanying repository.

\section{The Continuation Argument and an Explicit Threshold}
\label{app:contraction-proof}

This appendix elaborates the continuation step of \Cref{thm:wellposedness}(ii)--(iii)
and gives a concrete numerical illustration of $\delta_0$.

\subsection{Continuation: propagating the fourth moment}

For the continuation argument to apply at each stage, the terminal law of the local
equilibrium at the end of subinterval $k$ must itself satisfy \Cref{ass:initial}
(finite fourth moment) so that it is a valid initial condition for subinterval
$k+1$. This follows from a standard a priori moment estimate for the Lipschitz SDE
\eqref{eq:agent-sde}: under \Cref{ass:lipschitz-drift,ass:volatility}, if
$\mathbb{E}[|X_0|^4] < \infty$ then
\[
    \sup_{t\in[0,\delta]} \mathbb{E}\big[|X_t|^4\big] \le
    C\big(1+\mathbb{E}[|X_0|^4]\big)e^{C\delta}
\]
for a constant $C$ depending only on $L_b$, $\sigma$, $\|\sigma_0\|_F$ (a routine
application of It\^o's formula to $|X_t|^4$ and Gronwall's inequality, in the
standard, unproblematic differential form -- this bound does not involve the
self-referential structure that caused the error in \Cref{app:why-fails}, since it
concerns only the fourth moment of a single solution, not a difference of two
coupled solutions). Hence the fourth moment remains finite (with an explicit,
uniform-in-$k$ bound) across all $\lceil T/\delta_0 \rceil$ subintervals.

\subsection{Numerical illustration of $\delta_0$}

For the canonical LQ-MFG benchmark (Example~\ref{ex:lq-mfg}, baseline parameters
$\kappa=0.5,\lambda=1,c_1=2,c_2=5,\sigma=0.2,\sigma_0=0.3,T=1$),
\Cref{prop:lq-value} gives $L_b=\max(\kappa,1)=1$ and $C_\alpha=1/\lambda=1$
exactly. $C_{\mathrm{BSDE}}$ requires evaluating the cited BSDE estimate on the
linear adjoint driver $(-\kappa,-c_1)$; we use a representative value
$C_{\mathrm{BSDE}} \approx 2.0$, consistent with the magnitude expected for a
linear driver with these coefficients over $T=1$ (a precise value requires
tracking the exact constant in the cited theorem, which we do not carry out here).
With these values, formula
\eqref{eq:delta0} gives $\delta_0 \approx 2.6\times 10^{-3}$, so covering $T=1$
requires on the order of $380$ subintervals. This threshold is conservative -- a
consequence of the crude inequalities used throughout (in particular
$(a+b+c)^2\le 3(a^2+b^2+c^2)$, which is loose whenever the three terms are of very
different sizes) -- and is not intended as a practically tight step size for the
numerical scheme of Chapter 4 of \citet{monyimba2026thesis}, which uses a much
finer,
independently justified time discretisation. Sharpening $\delta_0$ towards a
practically useful threshold, e.g.\ via the displacement-monotonicity techniques of
\citet{jacksontangpi2024} or \citet{jacksonmeszaros2025} in place of the crude
Cauchy--Schwarz-type bounds used here, is identified as a natural direction for
future work.
