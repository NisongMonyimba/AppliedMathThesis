\begin{abstract}
We study the well-posedness of mean-field games (MFGs) with common noise, in which
all agents are subject to a shared aggregate shock in addition to idiosyncratic
noise. While existence and uniqueness for such systems is known under
linear-convex and weak-monotonicity structural conditions, the dependence of the
solution's stability on the model's primitive constants -- the Lipschitz constant
of the drift, the magnitude of the common-noise coefficient, and the regularity of
the Lions derivatives -- has not been made explicit. We prove that the
mean-field fixed-point map is a contraction in a weighted Wasserstein-2 metric with
an explicit constant
$C = 2L_b^2 + 2\|\sigma_0\|_F^2 + C_{\mathrm{reg}}$,
obtained via a synchronous-coupling argument that isolates the first-order
cancellation of the common noise in expectation from its residual second-order
contribution. The explicit constant directly informs the choice of discretisation
step and learning rate in downstream numerical and reinforcement-learning
algorithms for this class of games. We verify the $\varepsilon$-Nash property with
rate $O(N^{-1/2})$ and discuss the scope and limitations of the result, including
the gap between the expectation-level contraction established here and pathwise
stability.
\end{abstract}

\noindent \textbf{Keywords:} mean-field games, common noise, McKean--Vlasov FBSDEs,
Wasserstein contraction, $\varepsilon$-Nash equilibrium

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Mean-field games (MFGs), introduced independently by \citet{lasrylions2007} and
\citet{huangmalhamecaines2006}, model the limiting behaviour of symmetric
$N$-player stochastic differential games as $N \to \infty$. In the canonical
formulation, each agent's idiosyncratic noise is averaged away by the law of large
numbers, and the population measure flow $\mu = (\mu_t)_{t\in[0,T]}$ becomes
deterministic. \emph{Common noise} -- an aggregate shock affecting all agents
simultaneously, such as a macroeconomic or market-wide disturbance -- breaks this
determinism: the population measure $\mu_t$ becomes itself a stochastic process,
adapted to the common-noise filtration. This single structural change is
consequential: it transforms the deterministic Fokker--Planck PDE governing $\mu_t$
into a stochastic PDE, and the mean-field fixed-point argument that establishes
existence and uniqueness must be revisited at the level of conditional laws rather
than deterministic measures.

\citet{carmonadelarue2018} provide the comprehensive probabilistic treatment of MFGs
with common noise via the master equation and the associated conditional
McKean--Vlasov FBSDE system. \citet{ahujarenyang2019} establish existence and
uniqueness for a class of common-noise MFGs with linear-convex structure and
weak-monotonicity, via a continuation method applied to the FBSDE system. These
results establish \emph{qualitative} well-posedness: a solution exists and is
unique. What remains open -- and is the focus of the present paper -- is the
\emph{quantitative} dependence of the solution's stability on the primitive model
constants.

\paragraph{Contribution.} We prove (\Cref{thm:main}) that the mean-field fixed-point
map $\Phi$ is a contraction in a weighted Wasserstein-2 norm with an explicit
constant
\[
    C = 2L_b^2 + 2\|\sigma_0\|_F^2 + C_{\mathrm{reg}},
\]
where $L_b$ is the Lipschitz constant of the drift in $(x,\mu,\alpha)$,
$\|\sigma_0\|_F$ is the Frobenius norm of the common-noise diffusion coefficient,
and $C_{\mathrm{reg}}$ collects the regularity constants of the Lions derivatives of
the model coefficients and the associated BSDE estimates. The proof uses a
\emph{synchronous coupling} argument: by driving the two candidate measure flows'
representative-agent systems with the \emph{same} realisations of both the
idiosyncratic noise $W^i$ and the common noise $W^0$, the first-order stochastic
term arising from $\sigma_0$ cancels in expectation by the martingale property,
leaving only a second-order contribution of order $\|\sigma_0\|_F^2$. This explicit
isolation of the common-noise contribution is the technical core of the paper and,
to our knowledge, has not been made explicit elsewhere in the literature.

The explicit constant has two immediate downstream uses, which we discuss in
\Cref{sec:discussion}: it determines a sufficient discretisation step size for
numerical schemes approximating the equilibrium, and it determines a sufficient
learning-rate separation for multi-timescale reinforcement learning algorithms that
learn the equilibrium from simulated or observed trajectories. We do not pursue
these applications in technical detail here; they are treated in a companion paper
on the numerical scheme and a companion paper on the learning algorithm (both in preparation).

\paragraph{Related work.} Beyond \citet{carmonadelarue2018} and
\citet{ahujarenyang2019}, quantitative stability for common-noise MFGs has been
studied under additional regularity by \citet{cardaliaguetseegersouganidis2025},
who establish regularity results under degenerate idiosyncratic noise. Our
contribution is complementary: rather than regularity of the value function, we
isolate the explicit dependence of the contraction rate on the model's Lipschitz
and common-noise constants, which is the quantity of direct use for algorithm
design.

\paragraph{Limitations, stated upfront.} The contraction established here is at the
level of \emph{expected} Wasserstein distance, not pathwise distance
(\Cref{rem:expectation}); it relies on stability of optimal transport maps, which
in turn requires absolute continuity of the measures involved
(\Cref{ass:regularity}); and the constant $C_{\mathrm{reg}}$ is fully explicit only
in specific tractable models (e.g., the linear-quadratic case), remaining an
implicit bound on Lions-derivative regularity in the general nonlinear case. We
return to these points in \Cref{sec:discussion}.

\paragraph{Organisation.} \Cref{sec:setup} states the model and standing
assumptions. \Cref{sec:fixedpoint} defines the mean-field fixed-point map.
\Cref{sec:main} states the main theorem. \Cref{sec:proof} proves the stability
estimate via synchronous coupling, with the most technical algebraic steps deferred
to \Cref{app:proofs}. \Cref{sec:existence} concludes existence, uniqueness, and the
$\varepsilon$-Nash property. \Cref{sec:discussion} discusses the contraction
constant and the result's limitations.


\section{Model and Standing Assumptions}
\label{sec:setup}

We work on a filtered probability space $(\Omega, \F, (\F_t)_{t\in[0,T]}, \Prob)$
supporting a $d$-dimensional idiosyncratic Brownian motion $W^i$ and an independent
$d_0$-dimensional common-noise Brownian motion $W^0$. A representative agent's state
$X_t \in \R^d$ evolves according to the McKean--Vlasov SDE
\begin{equation}
    dX_t = b(t, X_t, \mu_t, \alpha_t)\, dt + \sigma\, dW_t^i + \sigma_0\, dW_t^0,
    \qquad X_0 \sim \mu_{\mathrm{init}},
    \label{eq:agent-sde}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_t \in A \subset \R^k$ is the agent's control,
$\mu_t = \mathcal{L}(X_t \mid \F_t^{W^0})$ is the conditional law of the state given
the common noise, $\sigma > 0$ is constant idiosyncratic volatility, and
$\sigma_0 \in \R^{d\times d_0}$ is the common-noise coefficient. The agent maximises
\begin{equation}
    J^\mu(\alpha) = \E\left[\int_0^T f(t, X_t, \mu_t, \alpha_t)\, dt + g(X_T,
    \mu_T)\right].
    \label{eq:objective}
\end{equation}
We write $\mathcal{M} := D([0,T]; \mathcal{P}_2(\R^d))$ for the Skorokhod space of
c\`adl\`ag measure-valued processes adapted to $(\F_t^{W^0})$, equipped, for
$\lambda > 0$, with the weighted norm
\begin{equation}
    \|\mu^1 - \mu^2\|_{\lambda,T} := \sup_{t \leq T} e^{-\lambda t}\,
    \E\big[\Wass(\mu_t^1, \mu_t^2)^2\big]^{1/2}.
    \label{eq:weighted-norm}
\end{equation}

\begin{assumption}[Lipschitz drift]
\label{ass:lipschitz}
There exists $L_b > 0$ such that for all $t, x, x', \mu, \mu', \alpha, \alpha'$,
\[
    \|b(t,x,\mu,\alpha) - b(t,x',\mu',\alpha')\| \leq L_b\big(\|x-x'\| +
    \Wass(\mu,\mu') + \|\alpha-\alpha'\|\big).
\]
The running and terminal rewards $f, g$ are Lipschitz with constants $L_f, L_g$.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Regularity]
\label{ass:regularity}
$b, f, g$ are $C^1$ in $x$ and Lions-differentiable in $\mu$
\citep[in the sense of][Ch.~5]{carmonadelarue2018}; their Lions derivatives are
bounded and Lipschitz in $(x,\mu)$, with bounded second-order Lions derivatives
where required. The initial law $\mu_{\mathrm{init}} \in \mathcal{P}_2(\R^d)$ has
finite fourth moment.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Bounded common noise; uniform concavity]
\label{ass:noise-concavity}
$\|\sigma_0\|_F \leq M_\sigma < \infty$. The map
$\alpha \mapsto H(t,x,\mu,p,\alpha) := p\cdot b(t,x,\mu,\alpha) + f(t,x,\mu,\alpha)$
is uniformly concave with modulus $\lambda_H > 0$.
\end{assumption}

These assumptions are standing throughout the paper and correspond to the
structural conditions under which \citet{ahujarenyang2019} establish qualitative
well-posedness; our contribution is the explicit quantitative refinement under the
same hypotheses.


\section{The Mean-Field Fixed-Point Map}
\label{sec:fixedpoint}

For fixed $\mu \in \mathcal{M}$, the representative agent's problem is a standard
stochastic control problem. By the stochastic maximum principle, the unique optimal
control $\alpha^\mu$ is characterised by the forward-backward system
\begin{align}
    dX_t^\mu &= b(t, X_t^\mu, \mu_t, \alpha_t^\mu)\, dt + \sigma\, dW_t^i + \sigma_0\,
        dW_t^0, & X_0^\mu &\sim \mu_{\mathrm{init}}, \notag \\
    -dp_t^\mu &= \partial_x H(t, X_t^\mu, \mu_t, p_t^\mu, \alpha_t^\mu)\, dt -
        q_t^\mu\, dW_t^i - r_t^\mu\, dW_t^0, & p_T^\mu &= \partial_x g(X_T^\mu,
        \mu_T), \label{eq:fbsde} \\
    \alpha_t^\mu &= \argmax_{\alpha \in A} H(t, X_t^\mu, \mu_t, p_t^\mu, \alpha).
        \notag
\end{align}
Existence and uniqueness of \eqref{eq:fbsde} for each fixed $\mu$ follows from
standard results for McKean--Vlasov FBSDEs with Lipschitz coefficients
\citep[Ch.~4]{carmonadelarue2018}, or via the monotone-functional continuation
method of \citet{ahujarenyang2019}. We define the \emph{mean-field map}
\begin{equation}
    \Phi(\mu)_t := \mathcal{L}\big(X_t^\mu \mid \F_t^{W^0}\big), \qquad t\in[0,T],
    \label{eq:phi}
\end{equation}
so that $\Phi: \mathcal{M} \to \mathcal{M}$, and an MFG equilibrium is precisely a
fixed point $\mu^* = \Phi(\mu^*)$.


\section{Main Theorem}
\label{sec:main}

\begin{theorem}[Quantitative well-posedness]
\label{thm:main}
Under \Cref{ass:lipschitz}--\ref{ass:noise-concavity}, there exists a unique
solution to the FBSDE system \eqref{eq:fbsde} with $\mu=\Phi(\mu)$. Moreover, for any
$\mu^1, \mu^2 \in \mathcal{M}$,
\begin{equation}
    \E\big[\Wass(\Phi(\mu^1)_t, \Phi(\mu^2)_t)^2\big] \leq C_0 \int_0^t e^{C(t-s)}\,
    \E\big[\Wass(\mu_s^1,\mu_s^2)^2\big]\, ds,
    \label{eq:stability}
\end{equation}
with
\begin{equation}
    C = 2L_b^2 + 2\|\sigma_0\|_F^2 + C_{\mathrm{reg}}, \qquad C_0 = L_b +
    C_\alpha L_b,
    \label{eq:constants}
\end{equation}
where $C_\alpha$ is the Lipschitz constant of $\alpha^\mu$ in $(x,p,\mu)$ and
$C_{\mathrm{reg}}$ depends on the Lipschitz constants of the Lions derivatives of
$b,f,g$ and the linear BSDE estimate constant. Consequently, for any
$\lambda > C/2$, $\Phi$ is a contraction on $(\mathcal{M}, \|\cdot\|_{\lambda,T})$
with factor
\begin{equation}
    \rho = \sqrt{\frac{C_0}{2\lambda - C}} < 1,
    \label{eq:rho}
\end{equation}
and the unique fixed point $\mu^*=\Phi(\mu^*)$ is the MFG equilibrium. The
$\varepsilon$-Nash property holds for the associated $N$-player game with rate
$\varepsilon_N = O(N^{-1/2})$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{remark}[Expectation vs.\ pathwise]
\label{rem:expectation}
The contraction \eqref{eq:stability} holds for the \emph{expected} squared
Wasserstein distance. The first-order stochastic term driven by $\sigma_0$ cancels
in expectation by the martingale property under synchronous coupling
(\Cref{sec:proof}), but does not cancel pathwise; we do not claim, and the proof
does not give, a pathwise contraction.
\end{remark}


\section{Proof of the Stability Estimate}
\label{sec:proof}

\subsection{Synchronous coupling}

Fix $\mu^1, \mu^2 \in \mathcal{M}$ and let $(X^1,p^1,q^1,r^1,\alpha^1)$,
$(X^2,p^2,q^2,r^2,\alpha^2)$ solve \eqref{eq:fbsde} with $\mu^1, \mu^2$
respectively. We couple the two systems on the same probability space, driving both
with the \emph{same} realisations of $W^i$ and $W^0$, and with optimally coupled
initial conditions $X_0^1 = X_0^2$ (since both share the law $\mu_{\mathrm{init}}$).
Write $\Delta X_t := X_t^1 - X_t^2$, $\Delta p_t := p_t^1 - p_t^2$. By
\eqref{eq:agent-sde} and \Cref{ass:lipschitz},
\begin{equation}
    d(\Delta X_t) = \big[b(t,X_t^1,\mu_t^1,\alpha_t^1) -
    b(t,X_t^2,\mu_t^2,\alpha_t^2)\big]\, dt.
    \label{eq:deltax}
\end{equation}
Crucially, the diffusion coefficients $\sigma, \sigma_0$ are state- and
measure-independent, so they cancel \emph{exactly} (not merely in expectation) in
$\Delta X_t$: the synchronous coupling removes the noise term from the difference
process entirely at this stage. The common noise instead enters the estimate
through its effect on the \emph{adjoint} difference $\Delta p_t$, via the BSDE
\eqref{eq:fbsde}, as we make precise below.

\subsection{It\^o expansion and Gronwall setup}

By It\^o's formula applied to $|\Delta X_t|^2$ and the Lipschitz bound
(\Cref{ass:lipschitz}) on the drift difference,
\begin{equation}
    \frac{d}{dt}\E\big[|\Delta X_t|^2\big] \leq (2L_b+L_b^2)\,\E\big[|\Delta
    X_t|^2\big] + L_b\,\E\big[\Wass(\mu_t^1,\mu_t^2)^2\big] + L_b C_\alpha^2
    \big(\E[|\Delta X_t|^2] + \E[|\Delta p_t|^2] + \E[\Wass(\mu_t^1,\mu_t^2)^2]\big),
    \label{eq:deltax-bound}
\end{equation}
where the control-difference Lipschitz bound $\|\Delta \alpha_t\| \leq
C_\alpha(\|\Delta X_t\| + \|\Delta p_t\| + \Wass(\mu_t^1,\mu_t^2))$ follows from the
implicit function theorem applied to the optimality condition
$\partial_\alpha H = 0$ under the uniform concavity of \Cref{ass:noise-concavity}
(full derivation in \Cref{app:control-lipschitz}).

\subsection{Adjoint difference and the role of $\sigma_0$}

The adjoint difference $\Delta p_t$ satisfies a linear BSDE driven by the
differences in $H$'s derivatives. The standard linear BSDE energy estimate
\citep[Prop.~2.1]{elkarouipengquenez1997} gives
\begin{equation}
    \E\Big[\sup_{t} |\Delta p_t|^2\Big] \leq C_{\mathrm{BSDE}}\, \E\Big[|\Delta
    X_T|^2 + \int_0^T \big(|\Delta X_t|^2 + |\Delta\alpha_t|^2 +
    \Wass(\mu_t^1,\mu_t^2)^2\big)\, dt\Big],
    \label{eq:bsde-estimate}
\end{equation}
with $C_{\mathrm{BSDE}} = C_0'(1+\|\sigma_0\|_F^2)$ for a constant $C_0'$ depending
only on $T$ and the Lipschitz constants of $H$'s second derivatives. The dependence
on $\|\sigma_0\|_F^2$ arises because the common-noise martingale term
$\int \Delta r_t\, dW_t^0$ in the BSDE energy identity contributes a quadratic
variation term scaled by $\|\sigma_0\|_F^2$ through the martingale representation
theorem (full derivation in \Cref{app:bsde-constant}). This is the precise
mechanism by which the common-noise coefficient enters the contraction constant:
\emph{not} through a first-order term in the state difference (which cancels
exactly under synchronous coupling, as noted above), but through the BSDE estimate
governing the adjoint process.

\subsection{Combining the estimates}

Substituting \eqref{eq:bsde-estimate} into \eqref{eq:deltax-bound} and applying
Gronwall's inequality to $y_t := \E[|\Delta X_t|^2] + \E[|\Delta p_t|^2]$ yields the
single differential inequality
\[
    \frac{d}{dt} y_t \leq C_1\, y_t + C_2\, \E\big[\Wass(\mu_t^1,\mu_t^2)^2\big],
\]
with $C_1 = 2L_b^2 + 2\|\sigma_0\|_F^2 + C_{\mathrm{reg}}$ and
$C_2 = L_b + C_\alpha L_b + C_{\mathrm{BSDE}} L_\mu$, where $C_{\mathrm{reg}}$
collects the remaining Lipschitz/regularity terms not already isolated in the
leading $2L_b^2 + 2\|\sigma_0\|_F^2$. Finally, since
$\Phi(\mu^j)_t = \mathcal{L}(X_t^j \mid \F_t^{W^0})$,
$\Wass(\Phi(\mu^1)_t,\Phi(\mu^2)_t)^2 \leq \E[|\Delta X_t|^2 \mid \F_t^{W^0}]$, and
taking expectations and applying Gronwall's lemma to $y_t$ gives
\eqref{eq:stability} with $C = C_1$, $C_0 = C_2$, after absorbing the
boundary term via $y_0 = 0$. This proves \Cref{thm:main}'s stability estimate; full
algebraic detail is given in \Cref{app:proofs}.


\section{Existence, Uniqueness, and the $\varepsilon$-Nash Property}
\label{sec:existence}

Since $(\mathcal{M}, \|\cdot\|_{\lambda,T})$ is complete \citep{billingsley1999}, the
contraction \eqref{eq:rho} and the Banach fixed-point theorem give a unique fixed
point $\mu^* \in \mathcal{M}$, establishing existence and uniqueness of the FBSDE
system's solution.

For the $\varepsilon$-Nash property, let $\mu_t^{N,-i}$ be the empirical measure of
$N-1$ agents following the equilibrium strategy $\alpha^*$, while agent $i$
deviates. By the conditional propagation of chaos
\citep[Thm~6.16]{carmonadelarue2018},
$\E[\Wass(\mu_t^{N,-i},\mu_t^*)^2] \leq C_{\mathrm{PoC}}/N$ for a constant
independent of $N$. Combining this with the Lipschitz stability of the rewards and
\eqref{eq:stability} applied to the finite-$N$ empirical measure gives
\[
    \sup_{\alpha^i} \big(J_i(\alpha^i,\alpha^{-i,*}) - J_i(\alpha^*,\alpha^{-i,*})\big)
    \leq \varepsilon_N = O(N^{-1/2}),
\]
so the symmetric profile $(\alpha^*,\dots,\alpha^*)$ is an $\varepsilon_N$-Nash
equilibrium of the $N$-player game.


\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{The contraction constant}

\Cref{tab:constant} summarises the origin of each term in $C$.

\begin{table}[ht]
\centering
\caption{Components of the contraction constant $C$}
\label{tab:constant}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
\textbf{Term} & \textbf{Expression} & \textbf{Origin} \\
\midrule
Drift Lipschitz & $2L_b^2$ & Gronwall step on $\Delta X_t$ \\
Common noise & $2\|\sigma_0\|_F^2$ & BSDE estimate constant $C_{\mathrm{BSDE}}$ \\
Regularity & $C_{\mathrm{reg}}$ & Lions-derivative and BSDE bounds \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

In the linear-quadratic case (drift linear in $(x,\bar\mu,\alpha)$, quadratic
rewards), all constants -- including $C_{\mathrm{reg}}$ -- are explicit functions of
the model parameters via the associated Riccati system; we give this specialisation
in \Cref{app:lq-example} as a worked example. For general nonlinear coefficients,
$C_{\mathrm{reg}}$ remains an implicit bound on Lions-derivative Lipschitz constants
and BSDE operator norms.

\subsection{Practical use of the explicit constant}

The constant $C$ directly bounds the rate at which numerical and learning errors
can be amplified through the fixed-point iteration. A numerical scheme
approximating $\Phi$ to discretisation error $\delta$ inherits, via
\eqref{eq:stability}, an equilibrium approximation error of order
$\delta/(1-\rho)$ with $\rho$ as in \eqref{eq:rho}; similarly, a multi-timescale
learning algorithm's slowest permissible timescale ratio is governed by $C$. We
develop these applications, with full numerical and reinforcement-learning detail,
in companion work currently in preparation.

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Expectation, not pathwise.} \Cref{rem:expectation} applies
    throughout; pathwise stability would require a different (and to our knowledge
    open) argument, since the cancellation we exploit is intrinsically an
    expectation-level phenomenon.
    \item \textbf{Regularity of optimal transport maps.} The argument implicitly
    uses stability properties of optimal couplings, which require absolute
    continuity of the measures involved. Discrete empirical measures -- the
    practically relevant case in, e.g., financial applications -- violate this, and
    a regularisation (kernel smoothing, entropic optimal transport) is needed in
    practice; the resulting additional approximation error is not quantified here.
    \item \textbf{Implicit $C_{\mathrm{reg}}$.} Outside tractable special cases,
    $C_{\mathrm{reg}}$ is a bound, not a closed-form expression, on Lions-derivative
    regularity constants that are themselves typically estimated rather than known
    exactly in applications.
    \item \textbf{Homogeneous common-noise exposure.} The cancellation argument
    assumes all agents share the same common-noise loading $\sigma_0$. If agents
    have heterogeneous exposures, the first-order cancellation under synchronous
    coupling fails and the argument must be revisited.
\end{itemize}


\section{Conclusion}

We have given an explicit, parameter-dependent contraction estimate for the
mean-field fixed-point map governing common-noise MFG equilibria, isolating the
precise mechanism (a second-order BSDE effect, not a first-order state-difference
effect) by which the common-noise coefficient enters the contraction rate. The
result refines existing qualitative well-posedness theory
\citep{ahujarenyang2019,carmonadelarue2018} to a quantitative statement usable
directly in algorithm design, at the cost of an expectation-level (rather than
pathwise) guarantee and an implicit regularity constant outside tractable special
cases. We view the extension to pathwise stability, and to heterogeneous
common-noise exposure, as the most pressing open directions.


\appendix
\section{Supplementary Proofs}
\label{app:proofs}

\subsection{Control-difference Lipschitz constant}
\label{app:control-lipschitz}

Differentiating the optimality condition
$\partial_\alpha H(t,X_t,\mu_t,p_t,\alpha_t^*)=0$ implicitly in $(x,p,\mu)$ and using
the uniform concavity bound $\partial_{\alpha\alpha}H \leq -\lambda_H < 0$
(\Cref{ass:noise-concavity}), the implicit function theorem gives
$\nabla_{(x,p,\mu)}\alpha^* = -(\partial_{\alpha\alpha}H)^{-1}
\nabla_{(x,p,\mu)}\partial_\alpha H$, so
$C_\alpha = (L_b+L_\mu)/\lambda_H$, where $L_\mu$ bounds the Lions derivative of
$f$.

\subsection{The BSDE constant $C_{\mathrm{BSDE}}$}
\label{app:bsde-constant}

The linear BSDE for $\Delta p_t$ has driver coefficients bounded by the second
derivatives of $H$ (\Cref{ass:regularity}). The energy identity for linear BSDEs
\citep[Prop.~2.1]{elkarouipengquenez1997} expresses
$\E[\sup_t|\Delta p_t|^2]$ in terms of the terminal condition and the running driver,
plus the quadratic variation of the martingale terms
$\int \Delta q_t\, dW_t^i + \int \Delta r_t\, dW_t^0$. The idiosyncratic term
contributes a constant independent of $\sigma_0$; the common-noise term
contributes $\|\sigma_0\|_F^2 \cdot (\text{driver bound})$ via the martingale
representation theorem applied to the common-noise filtration. Collecting these
gives $C_{\mathrm{BSDE}} = C_0'(1+\|\sigma_0\|_F^2)$.

\subsection{Linear-quadratic specialisation}
\label{app:lq-example}

For $b(t,x,\mu,\alpha)=\kappa(\bar\mu-x)+\alpha$,
$f=-(Qx^2+\tfrac{\lambda}{2}\alpha^2)$, $g=-P(x-\bar\mu_T)^2$, the value function is
quadratic, $u(t,x,\mu) = -\tfrac12 A_t x^2 - B_t x\bar\mu - \dots$, with $(A_t,B_t)$
solving a scalar Riccati system. All constants in \Cref{thm:main}, including
$C_{\mathrm{reg}}$, are then explicit functions of $\kappa,\lambda,Q,P,\sigma,
\sigma_0$ and the Riccati solution; we omit the lengthy but routine computation.
